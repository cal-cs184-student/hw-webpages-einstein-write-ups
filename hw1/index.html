Task 5: "Pixel sampling" for texture mapping

Pixel sampling is a process whereby the color of a pixel is determined as a function of a randomly sampled part of the pixel. In order to describe more detail the methodology used to determine the color of a pixel, I will go into how pixel sampling is implemented in our project. Here, pixel sampling is implemented by first setting up the triangle’s bounding box in order to determine the portion of the screen covered by the triangle: this involves computing the minimum and maximum values along its vertices in order to define its boundaries. Supersampling to reduce anti-aliasing is then performed; the process here is to subdivide the pixels in the bounding box into a grid of subpixels, iterate over them, and then use line equation tests to test whether or not they are in the triangle (a pixel is in the triangle iff all three of the functions in the test are positive). Barycentric weights are then computed from the line equations according to the formulas from lecture 5, which are then interpolated from the vertices’ texture coordinates. For nearest neighbor sampling, the interpolated coordinates are clamped to the [0,1] range, multiplied by the texture dimensions, and then rounded to the nearest integer. For bilinear sampling, (u,v) sampling subtracts 0.5 from each coordinate to align with texel centers; the integer coordinates and then fractional parts are computed according to the formulas from lecture 5. Then the four neighboring levels are fetched. Finally, once the texture color is fetched it is written in the supersample buffer at the correct index.

Task 6: "Level Sampling" with mipmaps for texture mapping

Level sampling is a technique used in texture mapping to dynamically select the appropriate level of detail from a mipmap based on how much a texture is being scaled on screen.When using linear level sampling, the final texture color is computed as a weighted average of samples taken from both the lower and upper mipmap levels, with the weights determined by the fractional part of the level value. It effectively minimizes aliasing artifacts and preserves texture detail by adapting the texture resolution to match the screen-space coverage, resulting in smoother transitions and a more visually appealing rendering outcome. In our implementation for task 6, we begin by computing the derivatives of the interpolated texture coordinates in the x and y directions. These derivatives, which represent how rapidly the texture coordinates change from one pixel to the next, are scaled by the width and height of the full-resolution texture. This scaling converts the change in normalized texture space into texel space, allowing us to estimate the number of texels covered by a single pixel. By taking the maximum of the magnitudes of these derivative vectors and applying a base-2 logarithm, we obtain a continuous mipmap level value that indicates the appropriate level of detail for that fragment. Depending on the user-selected level sampling method, our implementation either rounds this level value to the nearest integer for nearest-level sampling or uses it to interpolate between the two adjacent mipmap levels. In my workflow for this task, I used chat GPT after the changes weren’t significant enough when changing levels. Upon seeing its solution, I realized that I was clamping integers such that they were rounded to 0 all the time, so that we effectively weren’t switching between levels.

When comparing the four combinations, L_ZERO with P_NEAREST is the simplest and fastest because it always samples from the full-resolution texture using nearest neighbor interpolation. This minimizes computation and memory accesses since no additional mipmap level selection is performed, but it suffers from poor anti-aliasing, leading to visible aliasing artifacts when textures are minified. In contrast, L_ZERO with P_LINEAR still uses the full-resolution level but applies bilinear interpolation, resulting in smoother transitions between texels and improved visual quality, though it is slightly slower due to the extra arithmetic involved in the interpolation process. Moving on to L_NEAREST, this mode computes the appropriate mipmap level by evaluating the derivatives of the texture coordinates, and then rounds the computed level to the nearest integer before sampling. When combined with P_NEAREST, the renderer benefits from reduced aliasing through the use of pre-filtered, lower resolution mipmap levels, at the cost of additional derivative computations; however, the nearest neighbor method still remains relatively efficient. Finally, L_NEAREST with P_LINEAR takes advantage of both appropriate mipmap selection and bilinear filtering, resulting in even smoother, more continuous texture appearance and superior anti-aliasing, especially for minified textures. This combination, while providing the best visual quality among the four, incurs the highest computational cost due to both the derivative evaluations for level selection and the more intensive bilinear interpolation process, though memory usage remains largely similar across all methods since the full mipmap hierarchy is generated in every case.
